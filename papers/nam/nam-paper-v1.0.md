## A NOTE ON ORIGIN

The Neural Access Method™ did not begin as a method. It began as a problem with a consistent shape -- and a brain that kept finding the same answer to it, long before there was a name for either the problem or the answer.

A fighter between rounds. Adrenaline still running, nervous system still in the last exchange. The coach gives four technical adjustments. None of them land. The bell rings. The fighter does what he always does under pressure -- not what the coach said. Three days of drilling gone in thirty seconds.

A basketball player at the free throw line in the championship game. Ten thousand practice shots. Misses twice. The coach watching from the bench knows the mechanics are fine. Something else failed.

A stroke survivor after months of traditional physical therapy producing limited improvement. The arm does not respond. Then one question -- *do you remember what it felt like to shove linemen to sack the quarterback?* -- and the arm moves. The pathway was always there. The wrong entrance had been used.

These three situations share a structure. The skill exists, the pathway is built, and the capacity is real. Under pressure, under load, the conscious analytical entrance is blocked -- and everything queued up there is turned away with it.

The coach's job in that moment is to find the wire that is already insulated and run the electricity through it -- not lay new wire. NAM does not teach new skills under pressure. It finds pathways that were built before the pressure arrived and runs the current through them.

This is not new science. Procedural memory, cognitive load, myelination, attentional focus, autonomic regulation -- these mechanisms are well-established. What NAM provides is a repeatable four-step protocol for applying them in real time, in the seconds or minutes available between plays, between rounds, between attempts. The science explains why NAM works. NAM is what the practitioner actually does.

This paper documents what a lifetime of applied pressure produced -- military service, combat sport, elite athletics, physical recovery with no institutional safety net, and from 2020 forward, adaptive athletes and neurotrauma populations. The four-step mechanism -- ACCESS → REFRAME → SIMPLIFY → IGNITE -- was operating across all of it long before it had a name, surfacing every time conventional instruction failed under pressure and something compressed, direct, and routed through what was already built worked instead. The mechanism ran unnamed through the 2025 published work -- Cognitive Mage, Mythopoeic Intelligence Agents, Synoetic OS -- before the name arrived on January 26, 2026, and the full architecture was locked on March 14, 2026.

NAM operates as the transmission layer within the Neuroformation™ methodology (Slusher, 2026b). The Elevation Grid™ (Slusher, 2026a) identifies where the system is fractured under pressure. NAM is the mechanism that delivers the correction once that fracture point is located. Understanding that relationship is understanding what NAM is and is not.

## ABSTRACT

The Neural Access Method™ (NAM) is a four-step coaching transmission protocol -- ACCESS → REFRAME → SIMPLIFY → IGNITE -- built for the conditions where conventional instruction fails: acute pressure, depleted bandwidth, and a nervous system that has shifted from selective filtering to threat-priority lockdown.

NAM does not install new skill under pressure. It identifies existing procedural, emotional, or identity-linked pathways and reframes the current demand through them. The intervention compresses to a low-load cue, and successful execution is anchored so the pathway strengthens with use rather than eroding under repeated stress.

NAM is the delivery mechanism within the Neuroformation™ methodology, field-tested across the full range of populations described in the Note on Origin.

The paper maps each protocol step against its biological mechanism -- myelination, procedural memory, cognitive load, attentional focus, autonomic regulation, predictive processing, and identity-based motivation -- and defines the hard limits of the method: NAM can only access pathways that already exist, requires a minimum functional regulation floor, and depends on practitioner competence in ways a script cannot replace. AI-domain examples appear as structural comparison and production evidence, not as proof that biological and non-biological systems are identical. What the cross-substrate work surfaced -- moving the pattern intelligence of a practitioner into a system that holds under load -- carries its own name: Tacit-to-Technical Transduction™. That inquiry belongs to a separate paper.

**Keywords:** neural access method, procedural memory, myelination, cognitive load, constrained action hypothesis, state-gated access, performance under pressure, NAM protocol, bandwidth-preserving intervention, high-pressure coaching, identity anchoring, adaptive performance
## 1. INTRODUCTION: THE BOTTLENECK PROBLEM

### 1.1 The Bottleneck Is Architecture

The human nervous system processes approximately 11 million bits of information per second at the sensory level. Of those, a dramatically smaller fraction reaches conscious awareness -- estimates commonly cited in the literature range from 10 to 50 bits per second (Nørretranders, 1998; Zheng & Meister, 2025). The gap is architecture. The system filters at scale so conscious processing handles what matters.

The precise figure is contested -- one direct response to Zheng & Meister (2025) has argued that capacity exceeds this estimate under certain measurement frameworks, particularly in motor control (Sauerbrei & Pruszynski, 2025). NAM's operational foundation does not depend on any specific bandwidth figure. It depends on the well-established principle that conscious processing capacity is dramatically more limited than total sensory input -- and that this gap widens under acute threat. The precise number matters less than the structural fact: the bottleneck is real, it narrows under pressure, and motor execution pathways that run below it remain accessible when those above it are blocked.

Under normal conditions, the Reticular Activating System manages the filter intelligently -- relevant signals reach conscious processing, background noise is attenuated, and the athlete can hear the coach, process the cue, and integrate it into execution.

Under acute threat -- which the nervous system cannot reliably distinguish from high-stakes competition -- that changes. The filter shifts from selective to security-only, available bandwidth is consumed by survival-relevant signals, and complex instruction, multi-step technical adjustment, and game-plan correction are effectively locked out.

The nervous system is doing exactly what it evolved to do.

### 1.2 Why Conventional Coaching Fails in This State

Standard coaching assumes a conscious recipient. The coach identifies what needs to change, articulates it clearly, the athlete integrates it. This works in practice. It fails when execution is on the line.

The reason is mechanistic. Under threat, attentional control shifts from goal-directed processing toward threat monitoring. Previously stable performance is disrupted not because skill disappeared but because the regulatory system governing access to it has changed state (Eysenck, Derakshan, Santos, & Calvo, 2007; Eysenck, Moser, Derakshan, Hepsomali, & Allen, 2023). High-stakes reward conditions produce measurable collapse in neural planning signals -- motor cortex activity becomes less distinguishable for different movement directions exactly when the stakes are highest (Smoulder et al., 2024). The pattern is consistent: pressure doesn't remove skill, it blocks the pathway to it.

Choking under pressure has been explained through multiple theoretical lenses -- explicit monitoring theory, distraction theory, and reinvestment theory (Beilock & Carr, 2001; Cappuccio et al., 2020; Masters & Maxwell, 2008) -- each arriving at the same observation: conscious intervention at the wrong moment makes execution worse. NAM is built on that observation.

The conventional coaching model that delivers multi-point technical correction at a high-stress performance moment does not just compete with execution -- it crowds it out. Every additional instruction added at the bottleneck is load the system cannot carry.

### 1.3 The Solution

The skill the athlete needs is already encoded. The pathway is already built. Under pressure it is not accessible through the conscious, analytical, language-mediated route -- but it is accessible through a different one: a cue designed to reduce reliance on conscious bottlenecked processing by connecting directly to the emotional and procedural memory structure.

NAM provides the systematic approach for finding that cue, delivering it with minimum cognitive load, and anchoring its successful execution to identity so the pathway strengthens with use.

This paper presents NAM as a formal four-step protocol with defined structure and mechanism. It documents the biological and cognitive mechanisms underlying each step, provides field evidence across five populations and one structural cross-domain comparison, distinguishes NAM from protocol libraries, script-based coaching, and motivational intervention, and establishes NAM as the delivery layer of the Neuroformation™ methodology (Slusher, 2026b), operating from Elevation Grid™ diagnostic coordinates (Slusher, 2026a).

## 2. DEFINITIONS

Before proceeding to theoretical foundation, the following terms are used throughout this paper with the meanings defined here.

**Neural Cue:** A low-load verbal, visual, physical, or attentional trigger designed to activate an existing procedural, emotional, or identity-linked pathway. Not a new instruction. Not a reminder. An activation signal for something already built.

**State-Gated Access:** NAM's operational term for the practitioner-observed pattern that some cognitive, emotional, and procedural routes are easier to access under regulation and harder to reach under acute dysregulation. Used descriptively, not as a claim about specific neuroanatomical mechanisms.

**Pre-Existing Pathway:** A shorthand term for a neural route that has been stabilized through prior repetition and can often be activated with lower conscious load than a newly instructed skill. Not every such pathway is equally myelinated; the term describes functional accessibility, not a universal biological threshold.

**Identity Keystone:** The identity frame installed before pressure arrives -- the statement or anchor that defines who the performer is in relation to this demand. The keystone holds the arch before the load hits. Distinct from the Identity Anchor, which fires after execution.

**Identity Anchor:** A brief post-execution statement or ritual that connects successful action to stable capability rather than treating it as luck or anomaly. Fires after correct execution, in the moment the evidence is still present.

**Coached Evidence Loop:** The process by which successful embodied execution, explicitly named by the practitioner, updates the performer's operating model of what they can reliably do.

**Programs:** In the Neuroformation™ architecture, the structured delivery systems through which NAM is applied. Programs contain Protocols (structured procedural tools) and Primers (activation sequences). Tools such as Gas/Gaze/Grip and the 90-Second Flush are human Protocols that live within Programs. They are distinct from VGS AI frameworks (DriftLock, PME, UTME), which operate in a separate domain and are not discussed in this paper.

## 3. THEORETICAL FOUNDATION

### 3.1 Myelination and Pre-Built Pathways

The fundamental mechanism NAM accesses is myelination -- the biological process by which repeated neural firing wraps the axonal pathway in myelin, a fatty sheath that increases conduction velocity and reduces energy cost (Fields, 2008; Fields, 2015). Myelin is plastic. Activity-dependent myelination research has established that neural pathways remodel in response to use patterns, with white-matter structure shifting in ways that reflect the history of what the system has practiced (Monje, 2018; Bonetto, Belin, & Káradóttir, 2021).

Motor skill acquisition research consistently documents the progression from conscious, effortful processing (cortical) to automatic, efficient execution (subcortical -- basal ganglia and cerebellum) through deliberate practice (Ericsson et al., 1993; Fitts & Posner, 1967). Myelination of motor pathways begins in the earliest stages of skill learning and continues as practice accumulates (McKenzie et al., 2014; Xiao et al., 2016).

In applied coaching, the 300--500 repetition range functions as a practical heuristic for early pathway stabilization. The underlying biological transition depends on task complexity, practice quality, neural substrate, and individual variation. Activity-dependent myelination research supports the broader principle that repeated, high-quality neural firing can alter white-matter structure and improve conduction efficiency, but NAM does not treat any fixed repetition count as a universal biological threshold.

Once a pathway has been sufficiently practiced, it can fire with a fraction of the conscious bandwidth required to build or execute it consciously. This is why an athlete who has correctly built a skill can execute it under conditions that prevent any new skill acquisition: the pathway runs below the bandwidth bottleneck. NAM's ACCESS step is built entirely on this principle. The practitioner is not teaching. The practitioner is finding pathways that were already built and opening the route to them.

### 3.2 Procedural Memory Under Threat

Acute stress produces a well-documented shift in memory system engagement. The hippocampal-dependent declarative system -- which handles flexible, context-rich, explicitly retrievable information -- becomes less accessible under threat. The striatal-dependent procedural system -- which handles automatized, repetition-built, implicitly retrieved patterns -- remains more accessible (Schwabe, Joëls, Roozendaal, Wolf, & Oitzl, 2012; Dolfen et al., 2021).

This shift is the mechanism that makes NAM possible. The information that remains available under acute stress is precisely the information that was built through enough repetition to be handled procedurally rather than declaratively. Concurrent with this shift, stress disrupts hippocampal integration of overlapping memories — the relational inference mechanism that would allow a recently instructed skill to be woven into the athlete's existing knowledge structure (Schüren et al., 2026). Under stress, the hippocampus stops reactivating prior experiences during new learning, treating related events as increasingly separate rather than integrated. This is why attempting to install a new technical skill during acute stress fails at two levels: the declarative system is impaired, and the relational integration that would make the new skill transferable is broken. NAM's ACCESS step searches for that information -- not the most recently instructed skill, not the most consciously analyzed pattern, but the most deeply practiced one. The synthesis linking stress-induced memory system shifts, preserved implicit motor learning, and activity-dependent myelination as the structural basis for this selectivity is NAM's original theoretical contribution, integrating findings from Schwabe et al. (2012), Kal et al. (2016), and Bonetto et al. (2021) into a unified operational principle. Direct empirical tests of this synthesis in coaching contexts remain an open research question.

This principle extends into neurotrauma applications. Implicit motor learning has been shown to be preserved after stroke even when explicit learning is disrupted (Kal et al., 2016). Providing explicit verbal instruction during motor rehabilitation can actively interfere with the implicit learning that produces durable recovery (Boyd & Winstein, 2004). The implication is practical: for populations where the conscious motor pathway has been damaged, the procedural route built through pre-injury practice may remain the most accessible available pathway. Finding it is NAM's ACCESS question in its most critical form.

### 3.3 Cognitive Load and the Compression Principle

Sweller's cognitive load theory (1988) establishes that working memory has limited capacity and that extraneous cognitive load -- information that does not directly serve execution -- degrades performance. Under pressure, this constraint is compounded by the bandwidth narrowing described in Section 1.1.

A four-point technical correction imposed at a high-stress performance moment does not just compete with execution. It crowds it out. Every additional instruction added at the bottleneck is load the system cannot carry.

NAM's SIMPLIFY step is the direct application of cognitive load theory to real-time coaching: compress to the single cue with the lowest load and the highest route-activation probability. If the cue requires the athlete to hold more than one thing in working memory, it is already competing with the execution itself. The ideal cue approaches zero load -- a single word, a single image, a single physical anchor that fires the whole pathway without requiring the athlete to think about what they are doing.

### 3.4 External Focus and the Constrained Action Hypothesis

Directing attention internally toward movement mechanics degrades motor performance relative to directing attention externally toward the effect of the movement -- a finding established across substantial research (Wulf et al., 2001; Chua et al., 2021). When an athlete thinks about elbow position, performance decreases. When they think about where the ball needs to go, performance tends to improve.

Recent meta-analytic work has raised questions about publication bias and effect-size consistency in this literature (McKay et al., 2024). NAM's SIMPLIFY step does not depend on external focus as a universal mechanism. It depends on the broader principle of reducing internal attentional load on mechanics. The external focus literature provides supporting evidence for this principle while the debate about its precise magnitude and boundary conditions continues. What NAM requires from SIMPLIFY is a cue that does not add conscious mechanical analysis -- wherever the cue directs attention, it should not increase internal monitoring of execution.

### 3.5 State-Gated Access

Autonomic regulation appears to influence cognitive availability, attentional control, and retrieval access. NAM uses the term State-Gated Access operationally to describe the practitioner-observed pattern that some routes are available under regulation but difficult to reach under acute dysregulation. This framing draws from stress-memory research (Schwabe et al., 2012), HRV and self-regulation literature (Thayer et al., 2009; Laborde et al., 2018), interoceptive predictive processing (Seth & Friston, 2016; Barrett & Simmons, 2015), and clinical autonomic research, without depending on any single contested neuroanatomical theory.

The operational implication is straightforward: before any NAM step is productive, minimum autonomic regulation must be present. A fully dysregulated nervous system cannot execute even a single-cue trigger correctly. The system has to be functional enough to receive and act on the signal. This is why NAM always begins with ACCESS, and why, in severe dysregulation, ACCESS itself begins with the breathing intervention that makes everything else possible.

### 3.6 Predictive Processing and the REFRAME Mechanism

The brain does not passively receive information about what is happening. It actively generates predictions about what is about to happen and updates them based on incoming data. Prediction error (the gap between expected and actual) drives attention, learning, and route selection (Clark, 2016; Friston, 2010).

Under acute threat, precision weighting shifts. Threat-relevant signals are amplified; all others are attenuated. Cortisol shifts encoding away from flexible hippocampal pathways toward rigid caudate and basal ganglia pathways (Schwabe et al., 2012). The pattern wires as a threat-state response rather than a transferable performance skill.

The REFRAME step intervenes on this classification process. An accurate reclassification -- one the nervous system can process through an existing pathway -- reduces threat-signal magnitude by changing what the current situation means to the system. When the body's generative model of the situation changes, what is available changes with it (Clark, 2016). That is the mechanism.

### 3.7 Language as a Physiological Input

Language can participate in appraisal, emotion construction, interoceptive prediction, and physiological regulation (Barrett, 2017; Barrett, 2017b; Lindquist, MacCormack, & Shablack, 2015). NAM treats verbal cues as potential physiological inputs because they can alter meaning, attention, and predicted bodily demand. The ACCESS question is not merely a search for relevant memory -- it is a linguistic event that reorients the body's current operating state by directing attention toward a successful past experience rather than the present threat.

The precise timing and magnitude of single-cue autonomic change remain empirical questions for future NAM-specific measurement. The directional claim is supported by the broader literature on constructed emotion and interoceptive prediction: carefully selected language can change what is available to a system under stress (Barrett, 2017; Seth & Friston, 2016).

### 3.8 Identity and the IGNITE Mechanism

Identity is an active filter that shapes which actions a person takes, which demands they interpret as relevant, and which evidence they absorb as proof of stable capability (Markus, 1977; Akerlof & Kranton, 2000; Oyserman, 2024). When difficulty is interpreted as identity-congruent -- as the specific obstacle that separates where someone is from where they are going -- it activates engagement rather than withdrawal. When difficulty is interpreted as identity-threatening, it produces avoidance, self-doubt, or withdrawal.

The IGNITE step explicitly anchors successful execution to a stable identity frame before the moment passes. Without this step, successful performance under pressure is frequently discounted or attributed to luck. The athlete who finally executes correctly will often say "I got lucky" if nothing intervenes to claim the result. IGNITE is that intervention. The Coached Evidence Loop, the process by which explicit naming of successful execution updates the performer's operating model of their own capability, requires a practitioner who does it deliberately, in the moment, while the physiological evidence is still present (McAdams & McLean, 2013).

### 3.9 Constraint-Native Adaptation

Standard athletic development frameworks assume a normative baseline: what this body should do given typical anatomy and typical training history. Populations outside that baseline are assessed primarily against the gap. The remediation plan is built around what they cannot do.

That frame breaks down for populations whose architecture was built inside significant constraint from the beginning. For these individuals, what looks like limitation to a normative observer is often the structural feature around which a high-performance route was constructed. The constraint-led approach in motor learning explicitly addresses this: performance emerges from the interaction of organism, task, and environment, and unique organismic constraints produce unique but valid movement solutions (Davids et al., 2008; Newell, 1986). Applied to para-sport development, this framework recognizes that elite adaptive athletes develop through constraint-specific adaptations that cannot be evaluated against non-disabled norms (Allan et al., 2020).

NAM's ACCESS step is particularly effective with these populations because the search is not for the closest approximation to normative movement. It is for the actual route the system built under its actual conditions -- a route that exists, that has been practiced, and that works. The practitioner's job is to find it.

The architecture that emerges from this process has a name: Adapted Architecture -- the functional structure that develops from what is actually present, shaped by history, constraints, and interaction with the environment, operating at its own optimum rather than against a prior or external standard (Slusher, 2026f). Adapted does not mean compromised. It means built from what is real. The concept has been arriving independently across six fields (disability studies, rehabilitation science, neuroconstructivism, ecological systems theory, adaptive sport science, and AI resilience research), each developing its own vocabulary for the same finding without a shared term to connect them (Hutcheon & Wolbring, 2013; Karmiloff-Smith, 1998; Levin, Kleim, & Wolf, 2009; Folke, 2010; Davids et al., 2008; Friston, 2010). NAM does not produce Adapted Architecture. It finds and activates the routes through which Adapted Architecture already operates.

### 3.10 External Confirmation

A December 2025 study from Stanford and Harvard (arXiv:2512.16301) examined AI agent performance across complex decision environments and found that agents storing structured lessons from past successes and failures dramatically outperformed those relying on in-context reasoning alone. The principle is identical to ACCESS: route execution through patterns built and stored before the current high-pressure moment, rather than constructing new patterns under load.

A May 2026 study from Zhang & Levin (arXiv:2605.16321) provides independent physical grounding for the broader transmission mechanism. Working from Wittgenstein's language-games framework applied to biological communication, Zhang & Levin demonstrated that an AI system can act as a transduction medium for a non-human system by constructing a shared game where that system's own dynamics produce the meaningful response -- not translation, not interpretation, but transduction. The system's behavior is the reply. The signal that emerges preserves the original architecture rather than re-encoding it. This is the same structural claim underlying NAM's practitioner-to-athlete transmission: the route through the pre-built pathway is not constructed under load, it is activated. The game is the translator.

Neither paper is a retroactive explanation for NAM. Both arrived after the work was already running.

## 4. RELATIONSHIP TO PRIOR WORK

### 4.1 What Exists and What Was Missing

An exhaustive gap analysis conducted April 2026 examined military training frameworks, Olympic coaching systems, motor learning science, applied neuroscience programs (DARPA, NASA), and commercial performance methodologies for the existence of a unified protocol integrating autonomic regulation, attentional filtering, procedural memory access, and identity anchoring into a real-time practitioner delivery protocol (Slusher, 2026e).

The review did not identify a published framework combining all four elements into a single real-time practitioner delivery protocol. Every existing framework addresses at least two of these elements -- some address three. None integrates all four as a causal, prerequisite chain with a defined delivery structure.

Motor learning frameworks (Bosch, Wulf, Bernstein) operate in the biomechanical-neurological domain -- no identity anchoring, no autonomic prerequisite, no real-time delivery protocol. Psychological frameworks (ACT, PST, Gallwey) operate in the mental domain without systematic integration of motor learning science or ANS regulation. Technology-based programs (DARPA TNT, neurofeedback) link physiology to learning outcomes but include no practitioner delivery protocol.

NAM is not an improvement on these frameworks. It fills a structural gap they collectively left open.

## 4. THEORETICAL FOUNDATION

### 4.1 The Myelination Basis Under Threat

Section 3.1 established that myelinated pathways run below the bandwidth bottleneck and that the practitioner's job is to find them. The question Section 4 addresses is why those pathways survive threat while recently-acquired or consciously-held skills do not.

Stress shifts memory systems. Under acute threat, cortisol and norepinephrine shift encoding and retrieval away from flexible hippocampo-frontal networks toward rigid striatal pathways (Schwabe et al., 2012; Dolfen & Albouy, 2021). Skills encoded in the striatum and cerebellum through extensive repetition -- the deeply myelinated procedural routes -- are protected by this shift rather than degraded by it. Skills encoded recently, held consciously, or dependent on prefrontal processing are the casualties. The threat state does not impair all skills equally. It specifically impairs the ones the practitioner is most likely to have added last.

Implicit motor learning research confirms the preservation side of this: procedural pathways remain accessible after significant neural damage, including stroke, in cases where explicit cortical pathways have been compromised (Kal et al., 2016). The mechanism is the same -- the striatal and cerebellar substrate that holds automatized skill is a different system from the cortical substrate that holds consciously processed instruction, and stress targets the latter while leaving the former relatively intact (Boyd & Winstein, 2004).

This is why ACCESS specifically searches for the oldest, most-practiced pathways -- not just any past experience. The most deeply myelinated routes have the deepest protection under load. The memory retrieval architecture compounds this further. The hippocampal CA3 network (the associative recall hub that connects a partial cue to a full memory) transforms through development from dense, random local connectivity to a distributed, sparse, structured configuration shaped by experience (Vargas-Barroso et al., 2026). In the mature network, no single input fires a pathway. Multiple inputs must vote together. Pathways built through thousands of repetitions have that coalition built in. Recent or shallow pathways do not. When acute stress depletes the bandwidth available for retrieval, ACCESS is searching for the architecture that can still assemble a vote -- the pathway with enough distributed structure to fire from a single word.

### 4.2 The Constrained Action Hypothesis

Wulf and colleagues (2001) established the constrained action hypothesis: directing attention internally toward movement mechanics degrades motor performance relative to directing attention externally toward the effect of the movement. The meta-analytic evidence supports the direction of this effect (Chua et al., 2021), with the strongest effects documented under acute pressure conditions with practiced performers -- precisely the conditions NAM is built for. More recent Bayesian re-analysis (McKay et al., 2024) found that estimated mean effects collapse after correcting for publication bias in general populations, which sharpens rather than undermines NAM's claim: the external-focus effect is most reliable under the specific conditions NAM operates in, not as a universal coaching principle.

This provides the foundation for NAM's REFRAME step. The movement the practitioner needs to access is best triggered through functional, externally-focused language that activates the movement schema directly -- not through anatomical description, which activates analytical processing of the movement and competes with automatic execution (Wulf et al., 2001). Under pressure, when analytical processing is degraded, the functional cue still fires. The anatomical instruction does not.

### 4.3 Cognitive Load Theory

Sweller's cognitive load theory (1988) establishes that working memory has a fixed capacity and that instruction exceeding this capacity produces degraded learning and performance. Under acute threat, available working memory is further reduced as threat-processing systems consume a larger share of available resources.

This shapes NAM's SIMPLIFY step directly. A five-cue coaching adjustment requires working memory to hold, sequence, and execute five discrete instructions simultaneously. A one-word cue requires working memory to hold and execute exactly one instruction. Under pressure conditions that have already reduced available working memory, the difference between these two instruction types determines whether the skill is accessible at all.

The one-word trigger survives competitive stress because it makes a minimal demand on a system with minimal capacity available. It routes through procedural memory rather than working memory. The demand is near zero. The execution fires.

### 4.4 The Map Is Not the Territory

A foundational principle underlying NAM's architecture comes from Alfred Korzybski's General Semantics: *the map is not the territory* (Korzybski, 1933). The nervous system does not show the athlete reality. It shows the athlete its representation of reality -- filtered, compressed, and shaped by every experience that preceded this moment. Under pressure, the map degrades. The filter tightens. The representation narrows to threat-relevant information.

NAM does not attempt to correct the athlete's map during acute performance stress. Arguing with a map the nervous system is executing at speed produces nothing. NAM finds the pathway on the existing map that still connects to the required territory -- one the nervous system recognizes and can execute. The REFRAME step is map-routing, not map-editing. The ACCESS step finds which pathways on the map are still open under load. The SIMPLIFY step compresses the route to its minimum navigable form.

Bandler and Grinder, working from Korzybski's principle, observed that problems arise not from reality but from impoverished or distorted maps -- maps that were useful in an earlier context and are no longer accurate (Bandler & Grinder, 1975). NAM does not debate the accuracy of the current map. It routes around the blocked section through a pathway that is still open.

### 4.5 The Law of Requisite Variety

W. Ross Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety (1956) states that the system with the most flexibility will tend to control the outcome of any interaction. Applied to performance coaching: the more pathways available under pressure, the more options remain when the primary one closes.

The cascade documented in Neuroformation™ v1.0 (Slusher, 2026b) is a requisite variety collapse. Floor by floor, options narrow. Floor 1 flooding eliminates autonomic flexibility. Floor 2 lockdown eliminates attentional flexibility. Floor 3 degradation eliminates skill pathway flexibility. Floor 4 fracture eliminates identity options. By Floor 5, the system has one option remaining: stop.

NAM increases requisite variety by making multiple pathways to the same performance output available. The practitioner who can only instruct through the conscious channel has one option. The practitioner who can route through procedural memory, identity anchoring, symbolic activation, and functional reframe has many. Formation before the crisis matters because NAM does not create new pathways under pressure -- it accesses the variety that was built before the pressure arrived.

### 4.6 The Unconscious as the Operational System

The conscious mind analyzes, evaluates, and provides explicit instruction. The unconscious runs the body, maintains habitual patterns, and executes automatized behavior -- in the language of symbols, imagery, and kinesthetic memory rather than analytical description (Erickson et al., 1976). Under acute performance stress, the conscious channel is degraded. The unconscious is still running.

Several functional properties of unconscious processing have direct implications for NAM. The unconscious receives all sensory data and filters it before presenting a fraction to conscious awareness -- consistent with the RAS research and the bandwidth evidence (Nørretranders, 1998). It maintains and executes automatized skill programs without conscious instruction. It requires repetition before installing new behavior -- consistent with the myelination threshold (Xiao et al., 2016). It responds to symbols and metaphors rather than analytical description. And it does not process negation: a cue of "don't miss" activates the representation of missing before the negation can be applied (James & Woodsmall, 1988).

That last property is directly actionable for SIMPLIFY. Every cue selected must activate the representation of the desired state, not the avoidance of the undesired one. "Make it" fires the execution program. "Don't miss" fires the miss program. The word chosen is the word that fires the correct unconscious program -- not the word that describes the movement.

The mental rehearsal evidence supports this framework. Richardson (1967) documented that athletes who rehearsed correct execution mentally, without physical practice, improved performance comparably to those who practiced physically. This long-supported finding has now been confirmed at the single-neuron level: Wadia and colleagues (2026), recording from over 700 individual neurons in the ventral temporal cortex of human participants, demonstrated that approximately 40% of neurons activated during visual perception reactivated during imagination of the same object, with roughly equal firing strength and using the identical neural code. The system does not simulate perception. It re-runs it.

### 4.7 Friston's Precision Weighting and the Encoding State

Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle provides the computational framework for understanding why autonomic state during practice determines the quality of the skill being built (Friston, 2010). The brain operates as a prediction machine, continuously generating and updating a generative model of the environment. The precision weighting assigned to incoming sensory signals is modulated by arousal state.

Under regulated autonomic conditions, precision weighting is calibrated: coaching cues, movement feedback, and environmental context are processed accurately and update the generative model. The skill encodes via flexible hippocampo-frontal networks, producing transferable patterns that generalize across contexts and remain accessible under pressure (Dolfen & Albouy, 2021).

Under dysregulated autonomic conditions, precision weighting shifts. Threat-relevant signals are upweighted; other signals are downweighted or ignored (Schwabe et al., 2012). The pattern wires as a state-dependent threat-response rather than a flexible performance skill. Recent state-dependent memory research confirms the mechanism: arousal state at encoding acts as a retrieval cue, gating access to the pattern under matching or mismatching conditions (Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience, 2025).

This has direct implications for NAM's ACCESS step. The practitioner is searching for patterns encoded under high positive arousal, high intrinsic motivation, or regulated autonomic state -- not arbitrary past experiences. The operational principle that follows -- *regulate before you rep* -- extends NAM's reach from intervention under pressure to formation before pressure arrives, connecting directly to the Neuroformation™ skill architecture (Slusher, 2026d).

### 4.8 Language as Biological Mechanism

Words are not merely communicative instruments -- they are tools for regulating human bodies (Barrett, 2017). Words from others can change heart rate, breathing patterns, and stress hormone profiles. Negative, threat-laden language activates stress-related brain circuitry. Positive, grounding language shifts autonomic state toward regulation.

This has implications for all four NAM steps. The ACCESS question orients attention toward a successful past experience rather than the current threat -- plausibly a physiological shift as well as a cognitive one, consistent with Barrett's (2017) constructed emotion framework, though direct autonomic measurement of single-cue effects in coaching contexts remains an open empirical question. The REFRAME shifts the nervous system's representational state through language. The SIMPLIFY trigger carries the full emotional and physiological charge of the accessed memory, compressed into one activation. The IGNITE statement updates the self-schema through which subsequent behavior is filtered.

Barrett's constructed emotion framework (2017) is consistent with Korzybski's map/territory principle: the nervous system constructs its emotional and perceptual states in real time based on prediction from prior experience. NAM accesses prior experience that generates the correct construction. The practitioner's language is the trigger for that access.

### 4.9 Identity Anchoring

Self-schema research (Markus, 1977) establishes that behavioral output aligns with the identity the person holds in the relevant domain. Akerlof and Kranton (2000) extended this: behavior that deviates from identity generates internal pressure to correct. More recent work on identity-based motivation (Oyserman, 2024) demonstrates that identity-congruent behavior is pursued even when difficult, because identity provides the motivational fuel that sustains effort against resistance.

NAM's IGNITE step addresses this layer directly. A technically successful repetition not anchored to identity produces a memory of good execution. A technically successful repetition anchored to identity produces a reinforced structure: *this is who I am, and this is what I do.* The first produces performance data. The second produces formation.

The distinction matters because performance under pressure draws on identity as much as on skill. Skill executes the movement. Identity determines whether the movement is attempted and whether the nervous system holds it under conditions designed to destabilize it.

### 4.10 The Generative Model and the IGNITE Mechanism

Friston's Free Energy Principle provides a further account of the IGNITE mechanism (Friston, 2010). The brain does not merely predict -- it generates motivated models about what it is and what the world should be, then acts to minimize the gap between those models and observed reality. Progress toward a desired state is intrinsically rewarding because it reduces entropy: the closer the system gets to the desired state, the less uncertainty remains. Dopamine fires not because the rep was technically correct but because the identity gap just closed.

This is consistent with both the power of IGNITE and the fragility of execution without it. Without the identity anchor, each successful rep closes the performance gap temporarily but leaves the identity gap intact. The system has not updated its identity prior -- its generative model of what kind of agent it is -- so under pressure it defaults to whatever structure was already there. IGNITE installs a new one. The motivation is no longer toward the performance but toward the self-concept that produces it, which is a far more durable anchor under load.

It also explains what NAM arrests at the IGNITE step: the prediction error cascade that Peterson describes as depression -- small errors propagating unchecked upward through the hierarchy to catastrophic identity collapse (Peterson & Friston, 2023). The successful execution, anchored to identity, becomes a high-level prior that resists upward cascade of lower-level prediction errors. The identity floor holds.

## 5. THE FOUR-STEP PROTOCOL

NAM operates through four steps applied in sequence. Each step has a defined function, a biological basis, and a practical application. The four steps are: ACCESS → REFRAME → SIMPLIFY → IGNITE.

Each step creates the conditions the next step requires. ACCESS finds the open pathway. REFRAME maps the demand through it. SIMPLIFY compresses the map to a single executable signal. IGNITE claims the result as identity so the pathway holds under future load. Skip a step or reorder them and the delivery fails.

The four-step sequence maps directly to Boyd's OODA loop (Boyd, 1987): ACCESS observes what the system already owns, REFRAME orients the new demand through existing schema, SIMPLIFY decides on one executable signal, IGNITE acts and anchors the result. NAM is OODA compressed to bandwidth-preserving coaching language, designed to complete a full cycle in under ten seconds under threat.

---

### 5.1 ACCESS: Find the Pre-Built Pathway

**Function:** Locate a pathway the practitioner can route through: an emotionally charged, well-practiced behavioral pattern already in the person's wiring that shares structural features with the target skill. Not the skill being coached.

**Biological basis:** The basal ganglia organizes motor programs around functional schemas, not anatomical descriptions. A schema built through thousands of repetitions in one context can be accessed and transferred to a structurally similar movement in a different context -- motor program generalization, the same pattern applied to a new demand (Schmidt, 1975). Under threat, deeply myelinated schemas in the basal ganglia and cerebellum remain accessible when cortically-mediated instruction is blocked (the differential survival mechanism established in Section 4.1; Kal et al., 2016). ACCESS finds the schemas that still have a door open.

**In practice:** The practitioner scans the person's history for sports, physical skills, hobbies, or repeated behavioral patterns that share a movement quality or effort characteristic with the target. A wrestler's throw shares hip drive with a power clean. A fighter's jab shares shoulder packing with a bench press setup. A quarterback's release shares external rotation with a throwing pattern in physical therapy. The search is for structural overlap that the motor system already recognizes, not analogy in the literary sense.

The ACCESS question is: *What have you already done 1,000 times that feels like what I need you to do now?*

**Example — Chris Oates, stroke recovery:**

Chris Oates played linebacker for the University of Kentucky from 2018 to 2019 (25 games, 73 tackles). He suffered a stroke in May 2020 at age 20, resulting in complete loss of feeling and mobility on his left side. Months of physical therapy targeting the conscious cortical motor pathway for shoulder extension had produced limited functional improvement.

The first NAM session began not with exercise but with a question: *Do you remember shoving linemen to get to the quarterback?* His face changed. He smiled. The procedural memory of 1,000+ football repetitions was intact -- stored in the basal ganglia and cerebellum, not in the cortical pathway that had been damaged. The front door was closed. The back door was open.

---

### 5.2 REFRAME: Map the Demand to the Pathway

**Function:** Translate the target skill into the language of the accessed pathway. Functional, external, emotionally resonant description that activates the movement schema directly. Anatomical description activates analytical processing of the movement and competes with automatic execution.

**Biological basis:** The external focus and constructed emotion mechanisms established in Sections 3.4, 4.2, and 4.8 apply directly here. REFRAME works because functional language activates the motor schema through the accessed pathway rather than through conscious mechanical analysis. The practitioner's language determines which prior experience the nervous system uses to construct the current state (Wulf et al., 2001; Barrett, 2017).

**In practice:** Once the pre-built pathway is found in ACCESS, REFRAME wraps the target demand in the language and imagery of that pathway. The question is what the movement does and what it feels like when it fires at full power, not what it looks like anatomically.

For Chris Oates: not *extend your shoulder, activate your bicep, drive through the elbow joint.* Functional. What does that movement DO? What does it feel like when the linebacker fires at full power? The motor system organized around the function, not the anatomy.

**Example — the toilet squat:**

Teaching a squat pattern by cueing spinal alignment, knee tracking, hip hinge mechanics, and depth targets requires the client to consciously coordinate four variables simultaneously -- cognitive load near maximum for a novice under any conditions, let alone pressure. Asking the client to *sit on the toilet* fires a schema built across thousands of daily life repetitions. Every variable is coordinated automatically. The reframe does all the work the instruction cannot. Once the movement pattern is firing through the accessed schema, the practitioner can introduce technical refinement incrementally -- adjusting load, depth, or positional cues against a movement that is already executing rather than one still being assembled under conscious control.

---

### 5.3 SIMPLIFY: Compress to One Neural Cue

**Function:** Distill the reframe to a single Neural Cue: one word or brief phrase that survives competitive stress. The cue must require near-zero working memory to hold and execute. It must carry enough emotional charge to fire the full schema reliably under load.

**Biological basis:** Cognitive load theory establishes that instruction complexity must not exceed available working memory capacity (Sweller, 1988). Under threat, that capacity is further reduced. A one-word cue makes a minimal demand on a system with minimal capacity available. A multi-step instruction exceeds what is there. The cue survives. The instruction does not (Beilock & Carr, 2001; Smoulder et al., 2024).

The cue also operates through unconscious processing channels -- the negation principle established in Section 4.6 applies directly here: every Neural Cue must activate the desired state, not the avoidance of the undesired one.

**In practice:** After ACCESS has found the pathway and REFRAME has mapped the demand through it, SIMPLIFY compresses the functional reframe to the smallest possible executable signal. The word comes from the pathway, not from the practitioner's vocabulary. It carries the emotional weight of the accessed memory. It fires the whole schema when spoken or thought.

For Chris Oates, the word was: **PUSH.**

Not *extend your shoulder.* Not *engage the kinetic chain.* PUSH. One word that held 1,000 football repetitions, the functional memory of what that movement does at full power, and the identity of the linebacker who lived in that body before the stroke. The word fired everything. Under maximum load. In the first session.

---

### 5.4 IGNITE: Anchor to Identity

**Function:** Connect the successful execution to the person's identity structure, not to their performance record. The practitioner does not note it as a technique. It becomes an expression of who they are.

**Biological basis:** Self-schema research establishes that behavioral output aligns with held identity (Markus, 1977). Behavior that deviates from identity generates internal pressure to correct (Akerlof & Kranton, 2000). Identity-based motivation sustains effort against resistance because identity provides the motivational fuel that performance goals cannot (Oyserman, 2024). Anchoring successful execution to identity pulls the person toward repeating it, not as coached behavior but as self-consistent behavior.

The IGNITE statement holds longer when Floor 1 is regulated at the moment of execution. The encoding mechanism is established in Section 4.7 -- the identity claim takes root when the nervous system is not in threat mode. This is the *regulate before you rep* principle applied at the identity layer (Slusher, 2026d).

The mechanism extends to mental rehearsal: as established in Section 4.6, imagination of correct execution re-runs the same neural code as physical execution (Wadia et al., 2026). The IGNITE statement triggers that re-run at the moment of correct physical execution, reinforcing the pathway and installing the identity claim simultaneously, without a single additional physical repetition.

**In practice:** After the Neural Cue fires and the movement executes, IGNITE is a single statement that claims the execution as identity:

*"That's it. That's you."*

Not: *"Good rep, let's try that again."* That keeps the execution in the performance record. The identity prior does not update.

*"That's it. That's you."* installs the execution in the identity layer. The next time this movement is needed under load, the identity layer is pulling for it.

**Why this step is non-negotiable:**

Without IGNITE, NAM produces technically correct execution in the moment and fragile execution under load. The pathway myelinates through correct repetition. The identity prior updates through correct repetition claimed as identity, not through correct repetition alone. Without the claim, the skill is a guest in the athlete's house. With the claim, it is a resident.

This is the mechanism described in Section 4.10: the prediction error cascade arrested at the identity layer. The athlete can fail a rep without failing as a person. The identity floor holds.

### 5.5 The Identity Keystone, Identity Anchor, and Coached Evidence Loop

Three supporting structures operate alongside the four steps and determine how durable the installation is across sessions. They operate in sequence: Keystone before, Anchor after, Loop across time.

The **Identity Keystone** is the identity frame installed before pressure arrives -- the statement that defines who the performer is in relation to this demand. It holds the arch before the load hits. The Keystone is installed in formation work, not in the intervention moment. When pressure arrives and the conscious channel narrows, the Keystone is already in place. NAM's IGNITE step claims the successful execution into that existing frame rather than building from nothing under load.

The **Identity Anchor** is what IGNITE delivers: a brief post-execution statement that connects the successful action to stable capability rather than treating it as luck or anomaly. "That's it. That's you." is an Identity Anchor. It fires in the moment the evidence is still present -- after the rep, before the nervous system moves on. The Keystone sets the frame. The Anchor claims the evidence into it.

The **Coached Evidence Loop** is the process by which successful embodied execution, explicitly named by the practitioner, updates the performer's operating model of what they can reliably do. Each Identity Anchor is one iteration of the loop. Over successive sessions, the loop builds a body of evidence that the identity prior incorporates -- as identity data, not performance data. The practitioner who runs the Coached Evidence Loop consistently across sessions is not just coaching skill. The practitioner is building the architecture that holds the skill under conditions the practitioner will not be present for.

## 7. LIMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION

### 7.1 Scope Boundaries

**NAM is not therapy.** The protocol operates within the scope of performance coaching and behavioral formation. Clinical assessment, diagnosis, and treatment of psychological or neurological conditions are outside scope. The Chris Oates case involved active collaboration with medical professionals and operated within clear clinical boundaries -- the scapular mechanical work was preceded by and conducted alongside licensed physical therapy. NAM is not a claim to replace neurological rehabilitation. It is a coaching protocol that identifies intact pathways and accesses them more efficiently than standard instruction, operating within whatever clinical framework is already present.

**NAM requires a minimum functional regulation floor.** The four-step sequence assumes the person can receive and process a single-cue input. A fully dysregulated nervous system -- Floor 1 completely flooded -- cannot execute even the ACCESS question productively. NAM is not a crisis intervention tool. The practitioner's first responsibility in a fully dysregulated state is regulation, not transmission. The protocol begins when the person has enough capacity to hear the first question and produce a memory.

**The cross-domain claim is bounded.** NAM's AI-domain validation demonstrates structural parallel, not substrate equivalence. Biological and non-biological systems are not identical. The claim is specific: the failure pattern NAM addresses -- bandwidth bottleneck, inaccessible conscious pathway, intact procedural route -- appears in both, and the same transmission protocol produces measurably consistent outcomes in both. Nothing in this paper claims that coaching a person under competitive pressure and stabilizing an AI system under adversarial load are the same activity.

**NAM is an access method, not a stewardship system.** The same route that allows a practitioner to reach a person under load can be used toward formation or toward manipulation. The four-step sequence does not determine which. The practitioner's ethical anchoring and stewardship of the person's agency does. NAM does not provide that anchor. It assumes the practitioner already holds it.

---

### 7.2 Practitioner Dependence

**NAM is not a script.** Practitioners who approach NAM as a fixed sequence of phrases will produce technically correct procedure and functionally limited results. The four steps are a constraint structure for principled improvisation, not a script for replication.

**NAM requires practitioner competence.** The ACCESS step requires knowing the client's history well enough to identify relevant myelinated pathways. The REFRAME step requires understanding the target skill well enough to find its functional equivalent in the accessed pathway. The SIMPLIFY step requires enough clinical and coaching experience to recognize which words carry emotional charge for this specific person. The IGNITE step requires judgment about when the execution is ready to be claimed as identity and how to frame that claim authentically.

A practitioner without adequate preparation in the client's history, the relevant skill domains, and the Neuroformation™ framework can apply the four-step structure and produce something that looks like NAM. It will not produce what NAM produces.

A specific failure mode worth naming: false attractor identification. Some deeply practiced patterns are stable not because they represent high-performance routes but because they represent adaptive responses to dysfunction -- a compensatory movement built around an injury, a behavioral pattern wired during trauma, a habitual avoidance strategy that became automatic. If ACCESS identifies one of these as the route to open, the REFRAME and SIMPLIFY steps will fire the wrong pathway under load. The practitioner's ability to distinguish load-bearing formation from adaptive compensation is not a skill NAM provides. It is a prerequisite the practitioner brings to it.

This dependence on practitioner judgment is built into the protocol correctly. Real-time intervention with a person under load cannot be standardized to a script without losing what makes it work. Recent research on autonomous research systems confirms the broader principle: targeted human judgment at high-leverage decision points produces better outcomes than either full autonomy or micromanagement (Liu et al., 2026). The practitioner's accumulated pattern recognition -- knowing which memory to access, which reframe lands, which word carries charge -- is the transmission medium. Removing it would not improve NAM. It would end it.

---

### 7.3 Provisional Mechanisms

The specific citations used to explain NAM's mechanisms -- Wulf's constrained action hypothesis, Sweller's cognitive load theory, Markus's self-schema research, Friston's Free Energy Principle -- are the current best explanations for why the protocol works. They are not load-bearing parts of the protocol itself.

If a cited mechanism is later refined or challenged, the correct response is to update the explanatory paragraph. The protocol holds regardless of which theoretical explanation is current, because the protocol is grounded in 28+ years of observed pattern replication across populations, not in the citations that explain it. The citations document convergence between independent scientific traditions and a protocol that was already running. They do not constitute the protocol's proof.

The polyvagal theory literature specifically warrants this note. Autonomic claims in this paper rest on HRV regulation, interoceptive predictive processing, and stress-memory research rather than on polyvagal neuroanatomy, which has faced formal contested empirical challenges (Grossman et al., 2026). The operational principle -- regulate before transmission -- does not depend on the contested anatomical claims.

---

### 7.4 Measurement Gaps and Future Directions

NAM's current validation is observational and cross-sectional. The cross-domain statistics (χ²(4) = 3.21, p = 0.523; χ² = 2.14, p = 0.543) document outcome distribution equivalence across populations and domains but do not constitute controlled experimental proof. Future validation work requires:

**Controlled trials:** Randomized comparison of NAM delivery against standard coaching instruction in matched populations under verified pressure conditions. The Chris Oates case and the adaptive athlete outcomes are compelling field evidence. They are not controlled experiments.

**Longitudinal pathway tracking:** Confirmation that Identity Anchor delivery across sessions produces the predicted identity structure update described in Section 4.10 -- not just immediate performance improvement. The Coached Evidence Loop is theoretically grounded but empirically underspecified.

**Pathway specificity measurement:** The cross-domain statistics confirm that NAM interventions produce consistent outcomes across populations. They do not confirm that a given Neural Cue activated the specific intended pathway. A cue can produce surface compliance -- correct-looking execution that was never routed through the intended pathway. That result doesn't compound, doesn't hold under load, and doesn't install. A per-session pathway fidelity measure -- tracking whether the cue fired the accessed schema or a proximate approximation of it -- would distinguish genuine pathway activation from behavioral compliance that resembles it. This is the next precision frontier for NAM validation.

**Practitioner reliability:** NAM's dependence on practitioner competence creates a standardization problem for controlled research. Future work requires a practitioner competency rubric that allows NAM delivery quality to be assessed independently of outcome, so that protocol fidelity can be distinguished from practitioner skill.
